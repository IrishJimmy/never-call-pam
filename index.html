Never Trust PAM! Here is my True Story

📢 "Trust the Process?" — How PAM Failed Me at Every Step

By Jim Ward | July 2025

When a pipe burst in my ceiling in January 2024 and flooded my home, I thought the worst was over. I was wrong.

That disaster marked the start of a year-and-a-half nightmare—not because of the water damage, but because of the company I hired to help me recover: Public Adjusters of Michigan (PAM).

They promised to be my advocates. Instead, they delayed, miscommunicated, dodged responsibility, and nearly bankrupted me while I fought to get my home—and my life—back.

🚨 Red Flags from the Start

  • PAM failed to act quickly after the flood, allowing moisture to linger and damage to spread.
  • Their "mitigation" was a joke—insulation was left hanging, soaked drywall ignored, and they brushed off my concerns.
  • I asked questions. I pushed for updates. But PAM gave me radio silence for weeks—and eventually months.

🧾 Doing Their Job For Them

I paid PAM 10% of my entire claim so I wouldn't have to fight my insurance company alone. Instead, I:

  • Tracked every missing document.
  • Cleaned up hazardous messes.
  • Chased down receipts and reimbursements.
  • Contacted my insurance adjuster directly (even though I wasn’t supposed to).

I became the adjuster PAM was supposed to be.

📉 Missed Deadlines. Lost Money. No Accountability.

  • They missed key appointments, allowed vendors to walk off jobs, and didn’t show up to defend my claim.
  • They waited 2.5 weeks to act on my request to break my $6,250/month rental lease—costing me $3,000.
  • My credit cards maxed out. A lien was placed on my home. PAM still refused to provide my claim file unless subpoenaed.

When I pushed harder?

A PAM representative literally told me to “go f*** myself.” Yes, you read that correctly.

🔍 What I’ve Learned

PAM did not act in my best interest. They:

  • Ignored urgent requests and questions.
  • Made critical mistakes in documentation.
  • Gave false promises about coverage and deadlines.
  • Refused to cooperate with my attorney.
  • Mishandled funds—over $24,000 in ALE payments remain unaccounted for.

This wasn’t a one-time mistake. This was a pattern of indifference, incompetence, and mismanagement.
